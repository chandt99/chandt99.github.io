The UK Music Industry as a Growth Contributor

A collection of exogenous shocks has left the UK music industry precariously placed; COVID-19 stifled demand, Brexit increased export barriers, and the government has slashed arts funding. This data exploration will examine the UK music industry's significance to the economy and its potential as a growth contributor. This is particularly important as the government looks to reform education; highlighting the economic significance of the industry has the prospect of shifting the narrative away from arts funding as unproductive, towards it as a worthy investment.


COVID Recovery

Figure 1 shows the Gross Value Added to the UK economy of arts sectors and for all the UK between 2019-2022 (COVID-19). Despite dropping to the lowest of all sectors to 37% of its 2019 value, the music sector has seen the strongest recovery, now comparatively producing at well above the UK index.

Data: Taken from the DCMS's Experimental Official Statistics, using a 2019 index and converted to TIDY format (not in ONS API).

Sources: GVA Data.

Global Comparison

Figure 2 shows the UK as the 4th largest music industry, while Figure 3 exhibits the UK’s 10 largest music importers. These contextualise the size of the UK music industry, while also quantifying its contribution to the balance of payments: almost £500 million. Figure 3 clearly highlights this as a growing exporter industry, with positive annual percentage growth in most markets, including 61% in China.

Data: Scraped and cleaned from the BPI and Wikipedia using different methods in Google Colab.

Sources: Export Data, Global Data


UK Revenue Breakdown

Figure 4 shows a breakdown of the industry, valued at over £1.2 billion. Here we see the rise in streaming revenue and the continued importance of live performances.

Data: Taken from a BPI report, converted to TIDY form in Google Colab.

Sources: Revenue Data.

Streaming

With streaming the dominant aspect of the industry, Figure 5 is a bubble chart plotting a country's GDP/capita against its music streaming penetration rate. There appears to be a somewhat strong correlation between the two variables; regressing these produces an R statistic of 0.6. The UK is in the upper-left quadrant, suggesting it has a high penetration rate for its GDP/capita.

Data: The penetration data could not be downloaded as it was behind a paywall, so I scraped it from the webpage and converted it to TIDY format in Google Colab. To sort the countries into continents I experimented with the ChatGPT AI bot, asking it to produce a unique dataset of continents for this specific country list, ensuring I didn’t have to worry about mismatched country names from an external list. This was exported, matched, and merged with the penetration data.

Sources: Music Streaming Penetration Data, Population Data, GDP per Capita Data.


Live Performances: Microeconomic Impact

Using Google mobility data, Figure 6 shows changes in retail activity in London boroughs from a pre-pandemic baseline on days where live music was played at Wembley Stadium. Here, we can see values well above the other London Boroughs and the Brent baseline, suggesting hightened economic activity due to music events (although correlation isn't causation). To contextualise this, the heat map below shows every data point in the Google dataset, with these Wembley dates producing some of the highest single-day increases during the pandemic. This suggests the positive microeconomic effect of live music for communities during COVID-19 recovery.


Data: I processed and cleaned the full Google Mobility dataset comprising over 30,000 entries in Google Colab, before matching it with a map of London. The full dataset shows retail, transport, and other data for any day in London over the pandemic, but this would not work with the Vegalite date selector/slider, so I narrowed it.

Sources: Google Mobility Data, London Map.

Intangible Impact: Life Satisfaction

To see if music is correlated with improved life quality (suggesting it as a positive externality), Figure 8 attempts to see if listening to more music as a country is correlated to life satisfaction. Streaming penetration data is taken as a proxy for the amount of music a country listens to. There is little correlation.


Data: Life satisfaction data was taken from the OECD API allowing automation, cleaned in Google Colab and merged. The OECD API was used as it includes relatively comparable developed countries, controlling for more variables.

Sources: OECD API.

Conclusion

Performing well above the UK average following COVID recovery and increasingly exporting to emerging music markets has suggested the industry as growing and worthy of investment. The UK is at the forefront of the dominant streaming market, opening the possibility for further development in this field. Finally, there is some evidence of the importance of live music to local economies, showing macroeconomic and microeconomic value. The Google Mobility dataset offers the possibility of further research. With greater resources, a more complete study could catalogue the effect of live music throughout London, conducting a diff-in-diff experiment comparing activity on live music days to non-music days to quantify the added local value.
