Week 2: Altering Data

IMF Total Lending

The first chart exhibits the total IMF credit outstanding for members between 1984-2022 in SDRs (billions). This shows recent years as having the highest absolute values of outstanding IMF loans over the last 30 years, suggesting high risk and instability in the global economy.

However, this absolute value does not take in to account the overall growth in global GDP over the last 30 years.

IMF Total Lending Adjusted for GDP

The second chart has adjusted this data to account for the rise in Global GDP to show the IMF outstanding loan value as a percentage of Global GDP. Years with the highest percentage of IMF outstanding loan to global GDP suggest higher risk in the global economy.

Note: both IMF outstanding loan value and global GDP have been converted to current US Doillar values.

Conclusion: After adjusting for global GDP, 1984 is the 'riskiest' year in terms of IMF loan repayment while 2007 is the least risky.


Sources: IMF Credit, SDR Valuation.

Week 10/11: Machine Learning

Classifying British Football Clubs

This graph looks at to what extent attendances and squad market values predict the league a club is in, comparing this to the league the club is currently in. It can be argued that clubs with different predicted leagues to their actual are over or under performing compared to their supporter base/squad value.

The graph appears to show that attendance and squad value as a moderately good predictor of league placement. In the EPL, Brentford, Fulham, and Crystal Palace appear to be overperforming as they are comparable to Championship clubs in terms of attendances and squad value, while Bournemouth is comparable to League 1 clubs. Similar patterns can be seen with Championship clubs who are EPL sized, and League 1 clubs that should be performing at Championship level. As a further study, I used the model to predict where Scottish footballing giants Rangers and Celtic would be in if they were to be included in the English football system- two clubs which are often seen as more comparable to top Premier League clubs. The results of this can be seen in the Colab link, showing that they are indeed comparable to Premier League clubs in terms of attendances and squad value.

Clustering the Premier League and EFL

A common problem in English football (one that can be seen in the above graph as well) is the vast differences seen between top Premier League clubs and those at the bottom of the football pyramid. It is this kind of divergence that prompted talk of the European Super League. Clustering allows us to see general groupings across the English Football Leagues, and whether the traditional idea of a 'top 6' holds in terms of squad value and attendances.



The 4 groupings can be generally be seen as categorising smaller clubs in the bottom 3 tiers of English Football (groups 0 and 3), smaller Premier league clubs and other historically well supported clubs such as Sunderland and Derby (group 1), and top Premier League clubs, stretching beyond the traditional 'big 6' to include Newcastle and West Ham (group 2). Interestingly, Chelsea are relegated from their generally accepted place at the top of English football to the second grouping in this model, likely due to their relatively low average attendance.


The Google Colab file containing the classification and clustering can be found here.
